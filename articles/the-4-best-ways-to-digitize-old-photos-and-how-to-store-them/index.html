The 4 Best Ways to Digitize Old Photos (and How to Store Them)

Old printed photographs are irreplaceable. Whether they are childhood snapshots, parents' wedding albums, or grandparents' black-and-white portraits, physical prints are vulnerable to fading, water damage, fire, and simple deterioration over time. Digitizing them protects those memories permanently, makes them shareable with family, and opens up possibilities for restoration, printing, and creative projects that physical prints simply cannot offer.

Why Digitize Your Old Photos?

Beyond preservation, digitizing old photos unlocks a range of practical benefits:

  • Share instantly. Digital files can be emailed, posted in shared cloud folders, or uploaded to private family galleries within minutes.
  • Restore and enhance. AI-powered tools like Adobe Lightroom, Remini, and MyHeritage Photo Enhancer can repair faded colors, remove scratches, and sharpen blurry details in old prints.
  • Create personalized gifts. Digitized photos can be printed on mugs, blankets, canvas prints, and photo books — perfect for family gifts.
  • Protect against loss. A house fire, flood, or simple aging can destroy physical prints forever. Digital backups stored in multiple locations are essentially permanent.

Method 1: Flatbed Scanner (Best Quality)

A dedicated flatbed scanner is the gold standard for digitizing photos. Scanners like the Epson Perfection V600 or Canon CanoScan series produce high-resolution scans at 600 DPI or higher, capturing every detail of the original print with accurate color reproduction.

Best for: Large batches of standard prints, slides, and film negatives. Ideal when quality is the top priority.

How to use it:

  1. Clean the scanner glass with a lint-free cloth.
  2. Place prints face-down on the glass, aligning them with the corner guides.
  3. Set the scan resolution to at least 600 DPI for standard prints, 1200 DPI for small prints or negatives.
  4. Save as TIFF for archival quality or high-quality JPEG for everyday sharing.

Method 2: Smartphone Scanning App (Fastest and Most Convenient)

Apps like Google PhotoScan, Microsoft Lens, and Apple's built-in document scanner use multiple exposures and computational photography to eliminate glare from glossy prints. They are free, fast, and produce surprisingly good results for standard-sized prints.

Best for: Quickly digitizing a large collection when speed matters more than archival quality. Excellent for sharing on social media or sending to family.

Tips for better results:

  • Shoot in bright, even natural light without direct sunlight hitting the print.
  • Hold the phone parallel to the print to avoid perspective distortion.
  • Use Google PhotoScan's multi-shot mode to automatically remove glare from glossy surfaces.

Method 3: DSLR or Mirrorless Camera Copy Stand

Photographing prints with a dedicated camera on a copy stand or tripod produces results that rival a flatbed scanner, especially for large or oddly sized prints that don't fit on a scanner bed.

Best for: Large prints, panoramas, album pages, and fragile prints that cannot be placed face-down on a scanner.

Setup:

  1. Place the print on a flat, neutral surface under two identical light sources positioned at 45-degree angles to eliminate shadows and glare.
  2. Mount your camera directly overhead, parallel to the print.
  3. Use a macro lens or standard zoom at its sharpest aperture (typically f/8).
  4. Shoot in RAW and process in Lightroom to correct any color cast or exposure variation.

Method 4: Professional Digitization Service

If you have hundreds or thousands of prints, slides, or negatives, a professional scanning service is the most practical option. Services like ScanMyPhotos, Legacybox, and local photo labs accept large batches, scan at high resolution, and return your originals along with digital files on a USB drive or cloud download.

Best for: Large archives, film negatives, slides, and Super 8 film. Ideal when time is limited or the collection is too large to scan personally.

How to Store Your Digitized Photos

Digitizing is only half the job. Proper storage ensures your digital archive is as safe as the original prints — or safer.

  • Follow the 3-2-1 rule: Keep 3 copies of every file, on 2 different types of media (external hard drive + cloud), with 1 copy stored offsite.
  • Use cloud storage. Google Photos, iCloud, Amazon Photos, and Backblaze all offer reliable, affordable cloud backup with automatic syncing.
  • Organize before you store. Create a folder structure by year and event before uploading. Renaming files with descriptive names (e.g., "1985_Family_Christmas_001.jpg") makes searching far easier.
  • Save in TIFF for archival copies. TIFF files are lossless and preserve every detail. Keep JPEG copies for sharing and everyday use.

Practical Tips

  • Start with the most fragile or irreplaceable prints first — faded, damaged, or unique images that exist nowhere else.
  • Wear cotton gloves when handling old prints to prevent fingerprint oils from transferring to the surface.
  • Scan both the front and back of prints — notes, dates, and names written on the back are often as valuable as the image itself.
  • Use AI restoration tools after scanning to repair color fading, remove dust spots, and sharpen blurry details.

Final Thoughts

Digitizing old photographs is one of the most meaningful projects a family can undertake. Whether you use a flatbed scanner for archival quality, a smartphone app for speed, or a professional service for large collections, the important thing is to start before physical deterioration makes the decision for you.

FAQ

What resolution should I use to scan old photos? 600 DPI is the minimum recommended for standard 4×6 prints. Use 1200 DPI for small prints, slides, and film negatives. Higher resolution preserves more detail for future restoration and large-format reprinting.

Is Google PhotoScan good enough for archiving? For casual sharing and everyday use, yes. For archival purposes, a flatbed scanner at 600 DPI or higher produces significantly better results with more accurate color and finer detail.

How do I restore faded or damaged old photos after scanning? AI-powered tools like Adobe Lightroom's Denoise, Remini, and MyHeritage Photo Enhancer can automatically repair color fading, remove scratches, and sharpen blurry details. For severely damaged prints, a professional photo restoration service produces the best results.
