4:T127f,<p>The 4×6 print is the most common photo size in the world. It is the default output of most photo labs, the standard for family albums, and the size most digital cameras are optimized to produce. Yet with digital photography, many people have lost their intuitive sense of what printed sizes actually look like. This guide covers everything you need to know about the 4×6 format — in every unit of measurement — so you can print with confidence.</p>
<h2>What Is a 4×6 Photo?</h2>
<p>A 4×6 photo is a standard print size measured in inches: <strong>4 inches wide by 6 inches tall</strong> in portrait orientation, or <strong>6 inches wide by 4 inches tall</strong> in landscape orientation. It is also commonly referred to as postcard size, because it matches the dimensions of a standard postcard.</p>
<p>This size has been the default for consumer photo printing since the film era because it closely mirrors the 3:2 aspect ratio produced by most 35mm film cameras and the majority of modern digital camera sensors.</p>
<h2>4×6 in Every Measurement System</h2>
<div class="table-scroll"><table><thead><tr><th>Unit</th><th>Size</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Inches</td><td>4 × 6 in</td></tr><tr><td>Centimeters</td><td>10.16 × 15.24 cm</td></tr><tr><td>Millimeters</td><td>101.6 × 152.4 mm</td></tr><tr><td>Pixels at 300 PPI</td><td>1200 × 1800 px</td></tr><tr><td>Pixels at 125 PPI</td><td>500 × 750 px</td></tr></tbody></table></div>
<p><strong>300 PPI (pixels per inch) is the professional standard for print quality.</strong> At 300 PPI, a 4×6 print requires an image of at least 1200 × 1800 pixels. Images below this resolution will appear soft or pixelated when printed.</p>
<h2>How Does 4×6 Compare to Other Common Print Sizes?</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>4×6 vs. 5×7:</strong> A 5×7 is noticeably larger and works better for framed portraits. A 4×6 is ideal for albums and wallets.</li>
<li><strong>4×6 vs. 3×5:</strong> A 3×5 is slightly smaller and less common. It was used for older compact film cameras.</li>
<li><strong>4×6 vs. A6:</strong> An A6 sheet (105 × 148 mm) is slightly smaller than a 4×6 and is the European metric equivalent.</li>
<li><strong>4×6 vs. 4×4 (Instagram square):</strong> A square 4×4 print crops the long edge of a 4×6 and is popular for square-format social media images.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Aspect Ratio and Smartphone Photos</h2>
<p>The 4×6 format has a 3:2 aspect ratio. Most DSLR and mirrorless cameras produce images in 3:2 natively, meaning a 4×6 print will use the entire image without cropping.</p>
<p>Smartphones, however, typically shoot in a 4:3 ratio. If you print a smartphone photo at 4×6 without adjusting, the lab will either add white borders on the short edges or crop the image to fill the frame. Check your print order settings to choose which you prefer.</p>
<h2>Practical Tips for Printing 4×6 Photos</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Always export at 300 PPI</strong> for sharp, professional-quality prints. Most editing software (Lightroom, Photoshop) lets you set the output resolution in the export dialog.</li>
<li><strong>Check your aspect ratio before ordering.</strong> If your camera shoots in 4:3 (most smartphones), crop your image to 3:2 before sending it to the lab to control exactly what gets trimmed.</li>
<li><strong>Use sRGB color space</strong> for consumer photo labs. Most online printing services are calibrated for sRGB, and images in Adobe RGB or ProPhoto RGB may print with dull, desaturated colors.</li>
<li><strong>Order a test print first.</strong> Before printing an entire album, order a single 4×6 to check color accuracy, sharpness, and paper finish.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Final Thoughts</h2>
<p>The 4×6 print is a timeless, versatile format that works for everything from family albums to event prints to personalized gifts. Understanding its dimensions in pixels, centimeters, and inches — and knowing how your camera&#039;s aspect ratio interacts with it — ensures that every print you order looks exactly as you intended.</p>
<h2>FAQ</h2>
<p><strong>What resolution do I need for a sharp 4×6 print?</strong> A minimum of 1200 × 1800 pixels at 300 PPI. Images below 800 × 1200 pixels will begin to look soft or pixelated at 4×6 size.</p>
<p><strong>Will my smartphone photos print well at 4×6?</strong> Yes, in most cases. Modern smartphones produce images well above the minimum resolution for a 4×6 print. The main consideration is the aspect ratio — smartphones shoot in 4:3, so you may need to crop to 3:2 to avoid white borders.</p>
<p><strong>Is 4×6 the same as postcard size?</strong> Yes.
